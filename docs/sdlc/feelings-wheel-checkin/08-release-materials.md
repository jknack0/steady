# Feelings Wheel Check-in — Release Materials

## Feature Announcement (Customer-Facing)

Understanding how your ADHD patients feel between sessions has always been clinically valuable — and notoriously hard to capture in a way that's actually usable for this population. Today, STEADY introduces the Feelings Wheel Check-in, a new daily tracker field type built on Willcox's validated seven-core emotion taxonomy. Participants can log up to three emotions per check-in with a single tap, or drill through secondary and tertiary tiers to name something more specific — from "Happy" all the way to "Hopeful" or "Optimistic." The entire flow takes under ten seconds on the quick path, which means patients with ADHD will actually complete it.

For clinicians, this translates directly into richer session preparation. Your STEADY dashboard now surfaces emotion trend visualizations across your caseload — you can see at a glance whether a participant's check-ins have skewed toward fear or irritability over the past two weeks, spot correlations with tracker completion rates or session attendance, and walk into each appointment with evidence rather than assumptions. Because the field lives inside the existing Daily Tracker system, there is nothing new to configure. Add a Feelings Wheel field to any tracker you already have running, and historical entries begin accumulating immediately.

Emotion data is stored as compact taxonomy identifiers — never as free-text strings — so there is no PII exposure risk and no change to your HIPAA compliance posture. The same audit logging and access controls that govern all STEADY clinical data apply here. This is meaningful emotional intelligence infrastructure that respects both clinical rigor and the real cognitive constraints your participants are managing every day.

## Key Talking Points

- **Built for ADHD brains, not against them.** The quick path — one tap on a core emotion — takes under ten seconds. Participants who would abandon a mood rating scale still complete the Feelings Wheel because the minimum viable interaction is genuinely minimal.

- **130 emotions, three tiers, zero new setup.** The full Willcox taxonomy covers seven core feelings with secondary and tertiary granularity. Clinicians who want depth get it; participants who need speed aren't forced through it. - **Validated clinical foundation.** The Feelings Wheel is grounded in Willcox's peer-reviewed emotional taxonomy, not a proprietary label system. That means clinicians can reference it in clinical documentation and discuss it with supervisors without needing to explain a custom framework.

- **Scales across a caseload.** Aggregate emotion trend views let clinicians spot population-level patterns — useful for group program design, outcome reporting to payers, and practice-level quality improvement conversations.

## Clinician FAQ

**Q: Do I need to set up anything new to use the Feelings Wheel Check-in?**
A: No. The Feelings Wheel is a new field type inside the Daily Tracker system you are already using. Open any tracker in your program editor, add a Feelings Wheel field, and participants will see it in their next check-in. No new modules, no new program structures, no reconfiguration of existing trackers required.

**Q: Which emotion taxonomy does this use, and why does that matter clinically?**
A: STEADY uses Willcox's Feelings Wheel, which organizes emotions into seven core categories — Happy, Sad, Angry, Fearful, Disgusted, Surprised, and Bad — with secondary and tertiary tiers totaling approximately 130 distinct emotion labels. Because it is a published, peer-reviewed framework rather than a proprietary system, you can reference patient emotion data in clinical notes, treatment summaries, and supervision conversations using terminology that is recognized across disciplines.

**Q: What exactly does the participant experience look like?**
A: On the mobile app, participants see the Feelings Wheel field in their daily check-in. They can tap a core emotion and submit in under ten seconds, or tap through to a secondary or tertiary label if they want more precision. They can select up to three emotions per check-in. The interaction is designed to be completable during a transition moment — waiting for coffee, between tasks — rather than requiring a dedicated block of time.

**Q: How do I see the emotion data on my end?**
A: Your clinician dashboard includes emotion trend visualizations for each participant. You can see which emotions have been logged most frequently, how the distribution shifts over time, and how emotional patterns correlate with other tracker activity. You can review this before sessions to prepare agenda items grounded in objective data rather than patient recall.

**Q: Is this data HIPAA-compliant? Does logging emotions create any new compliance exposure?**
A: Emotion selections are stored as taxonomy IDs — structured identifiers from the Willcox framework — not as free-text strings. This means there is no unstructured PHI, no inference risk, and no change to your existing HIPAA compliance posture on STEADY. The same audit logging, access controls, and encryption that cover all other clinical data in the platform apply to emotion data as well.

**Q: Can I make the Feelings Wheel field required so participants cannot skip it?**
A: You configure the Feelings Wheel field the same way you configure any other tracker field, including required vs. optional settings. If daily emotional check-in is a clinical priority for a particular participant or program, you can mark it required within the tracker.

**Q: Will this work for participants who are not verbally expressive or who struggle with emotional vocabulary?**
A: That is one of the primary clinical motivations for this feature. The visual, tiered structure of the Feelings Wheel is often more accessible than open-ended mood prompts for participants with alexithymia, language processing differences, or limited emotional vocabulary — all of which are disproportionately common in the ADHD population. Participants do not need to describe what they feel in their own words; they navigate a structured taxonomy and select what fits.

**Q: Can I use emotion trend data in outcome reporting or insurance documentation?**
A: STEADY exports and reporting features apply to Feelings Wheel data as they do to other tracker fields. Because the taxonomy is a recognized clinical framework, patterns in emotion data can reasonably support clinical narratives in session notes and treatment summaries. Always apply your own professional judgment about what is appropriate to include in payer or supervision documentation.

**Q: What if a participant selects an emotion that warrants a clinical response — is there any alerting?**
A: The current release surfaces emotion trend data to clinicians on the dashboard for review. There is no automated clinical alerting tied to specific emotion selections in this release. You should continue to apply your standard clinical protocols for between-session monitoring and crisis response. If a participant's emotion patterns are a concern, the trend visualization gives you a factual basis for raising it directly in session.

**Q: Does adding a Feelings Wheel field affect participants who are already mid-program?**
A: No. You can add a Feelings Wheel field to an existing tracker at any point in a running program. Participants will see the new field starting with their next check-in. Previous check-in history is preserved as-is — there is no retroactive impact on completed entries or progress records.

## Value Propositions

### For Clinicians

Emotion tracking is well-established in ADHD treatment but historically difficult to operationalize because the data collection tools are too burdensome for this population. The Feelings Wheel Check-in gives clinicians structured, longitudinal emotion data without requiring patients to fill out an instrument. Trend visualizations on the dashboard mean that session preparation can shift from "what has the patient been feeling?" — a question that working memory deficits make unreliable — to "here is what the data shows, let's explore it together." For clinicians practicing CBT, ACT, or DBT with ADHD patients, this is a meaningful upgrade to the between-session feedback loop.

### For Participants

ADHD is associated with emotional dysregulation as a core feature, not a comorbidity. Many participants struggle to name what they are feeling in the moment, let alone recall it accurately a week later when the clinician asks. The Feelings Wheel gives participants a structured, low-friction way to register their emotional state daily — in under ten seconds on the fast path. Over time, the act of regularly engaging with an emotion taxonomy can itself build emotional vocabulary and self-awareness. Participants are not just logging data; they are practicing a clinical skill every time they check in.

### For Practice Owners

Differentiated clinical data collection is increasingly relevant to payer and accreditation conversations. The Feelings Wheel Check-in adds a validated emotional intelligence layer to STEADY's existing RTM and engagement data — strengthening the case that digital treatment programs on STEADY represent evidence-informed, outcomes-oriented care. For practices building group programs or population health reporting, aggregate emotion trend data provides a dimension of outcome evidence that most competing platforms cannot offer. It also reduces session overhead: clinicians who arrive with emotional trend data in hand run more efficient appointments, which directly affects throughput and billing.

## Competitive Positioning

Most digital mental health platforms that include emotion tracking rely on one of two approaches: numeric mood ratings (1-10 scales) or open-ended free-text input. Mood ratings are low-effort but clinically shallow — a "4 out of 10" tells a clinician very little about what a patient actually experienced. Free-text is richer but creates PHI handling complexity, is difficult to visualize as a trend, and is a significant burden for ADHD participants who struggle with written expression.

STEADY's Feelings Wheel Check-in takes a third path: a structured vocabulary that is simultaneously fast enough for ADHD completion rates and granular enough to be clinically meaningful. The Willcox framework is not a proprietary invention — it is a recognized taxonomy that clinicians across disciplines already know. That means STEADY is not asking practices to adopt a new framework; it is making an existing clinical tool frictionless to deploy at scale.

Competing platforms in the ADHD and behavioral health space — including general-purpose EHR patient portals, standalone mood tracking apps integrated via workaround, and newer digital therapeutics platforms — do not combine structured emotion taxonomy, mobile-first ADHD-optimized UX, longitudinal trend visualization, and HIPAA-native storage in a single feature. Practices currently stitching together a mood app, a check-in survey, and a manual chart review workflow can consolidate that into STEADY's daily tracker system with a single field addition. The competitive message is straightforward: this is the emotion tracking capability your population needs, inside the compliance and workflow infrastructure you already have.
